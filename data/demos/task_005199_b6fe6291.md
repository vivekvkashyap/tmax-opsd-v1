This is an active-learning / adaptive-sampling problem, not a fitting problem — the naive uniform 150-point mesh will fail the MSE threshold because sharp features get under-resolved. First parse `/app/noisy_observations.dat` (CSV-like: id,x,y,timestamp) and use it only to *estimate* where the underlying function is changing fastest — e.g. bin x into many small intervals, smooth/average the noisy y within each bin, and look at consecutive differences or local variance to flag high-gradient regions. Concentrate a large share of your 150 mesh points in those flagged regions (denser sampling near peaks/sharp transitions) while still covering the whole [0,1] domain, rather than spacing points uniformly. Write your 150 x-values to a file, one per line, and query `/app/simulator </path/to/mesh.txt` (per its documented file-argument interface) to get exact y-values — never guess the ground-truth function yourself. Then write `/home/user/surrogate.cpp`, doing piecewise-linear interpolation between your 150 (x,y) points evaluated at the 10,000 uniform points `i/9999.0`, saving predictions to `/home/user/predictions.txt`. Finally compute the mean of the 10,000 predictions and derive a 95% CI via 1,000 bootstrap resamples-with-replacement of that vector, writing `lower,upper` to `/home/user/bootstrap_ci.txt`.
