This is an empirical signal-classification problem — don't guess a fixed threshold, derive it by studying the labeled corpora with the actual tool first.

1. Run `/app/extract_signal` on several files from both `/app/corpus/clean/` and `/app/corpus/evil/`, save the output signals, and inspect them (e.g. plot or compute FFT magnitude) to see how clean vs. evil signals differ in the high-frequency band — this tells you what statistic and threshold actually separate the classes, rather than assuming one.
2. In your classifier (a Python/numpy helper called from the bash script), compute the FFT of each signal, sum squared magnitude for frequency bins with `f > 0.25` cycles/sample (normalized frequency, i.e. bin_index/N > 0.25), and treat this as the high-frequency energy per window.
3. Bootstrap resample the per-window high-frequency energy values (>=1000 resamples, with replacement) to get a distribution of the mean; take the 2.5th percentile as your lower bound. Use the clean/evil corpora to calibrate what lower-bound cutoff correctly separates them 100%.
4. In `/home/user/filter.sh`: accept `<input_dir> <output_dir>`, use `xargs -P` (or GNU parallel) to fan out per-file processing, and copy each file to the output dir only if it passes your calibrated bootstrap-lower-bound check. Test against `/app/corpus/clean/` and `/app/corpus/evil/` directly to confirm 100% accuracy before considering it done.
