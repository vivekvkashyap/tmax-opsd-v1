Look specifically for a per-byte XOR/ADD-style operation applied in a tight loop over the input, and separately check whether the program accumulates a running sum/checksum of the bytes it processes — this is often appended after the transformed data, so compare `ls -l` sizes of an encoded sample vs. a raw input of known length to spot a fixed trailing overhead (e.g. 4 extra bytes). Confirm your hypothesis empirically: run the binary on a small known input, dump the output in hex (`xxd`), and manually invert your guessed transform on the first bytes to see if you can recover the original text; then verify the trailing bytes equal a checksum of the recovered bytes. Only once the algorithm and checksum placement are nailed down should you write `detector.c`: read the file, split off the trailing checksum, invert the per-byte transform over the remainder, recompute the checksum with 8/32-bit arithmetic matching what you observed, and compare. Test against both corpora before finalizing, since off-by-one wraparound bugs are the most common failure mode here.
