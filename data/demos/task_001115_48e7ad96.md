First read the WAV with a proven library (libsndfile, or shell out to a Python/`soxi`/`ffprobe` helper) rather than hand-rolling a WAV parser — get raw samples and the sample rate into an array you trust. Because the two frequencies are close together, a naive least-squares fit is ill-conditioned: seed your optimizer with a rough estimate from an FFT peak (or two nearby peaks) rather than an arbitrary guess, and prefer a robust nonlinear solver (Levenberg-Marquardt via a library, or a well-damped Nelder-Mead with multiple restarts) over plain gradient descent, since the loss surface has a narrow, curved valley near the near-degenerate frequencies. Fit the 1-component baseline the same way and compute the F-statistic from the residual sum of squares of both fits with the correct degrees-of-freedom difference. For the bootstrap, resample residuals (not raw data) and refit at least 100 times, reusing your converged 2-component parameters as the initial guess each time so it doesn't drift into a different local optimum. Finally, write all nine required datasets to the HDF5 file at the exact root-level paths, and sanity-check your reconstruction's amplitude scale matches the input signal's convention before declaring success.
