Second, write a bash driver that parallelizes per-frame analysis across frames with GNU `parallel` or `xargs -P N`, calling out to a small script/Python one-liner per frame that loads it grayscale (e.g. PIL/`convert` to compute a histogram, or Python+numpy `imageio.imread(..., mode='L')`) and computes density = (count of pixels with intensity strictly > 128) / total pixel count — get the strict inequality and grayscale conversion right, since off-by-one or using RGB channels instead of luminance will bias the fit. Third, fit `scipy.optimize.curve_fit` to the logistic form `D(t) = K / (1 + ((K-D0)/D0) * exp(-r*t))` over your (t, density) pairs — give it a decent initial guess (e.g. K≈max(density), D0≈density[0], r≈0.1) since logistic fits are sensitive to starting point and may otherwise not converge. Finally, expose a tiny HTTP server (`http.server` subclass or Flask) on `127.0.0.1:8080` with `GET /model` returning the three fitted floats as JSON, and keep the process alive in the background (e.g. `nohup ... &`) rather than exiting after fitting.
